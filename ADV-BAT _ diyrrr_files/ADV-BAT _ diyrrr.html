ADVISOR BATTERY REVIVAL

1 Before you start

Warning

Lithium Ion (Li-Ion) batteries can be dangerous if mishandled. The following content is provided as informative support for retrieving Li-Ion batteries and should only be followed by those experienced in battery handling. If you are not qualified or do not understand the risks associated with handling Li-Ion batteries, DO NOT ATTEMPT TO RETRIEVE THEM. Always wear appropriate personal protective equipment and follow all manufacturer instructions when handling Li-Ion batteries. Failure to do so may result in serious injury or death. 

Attention

Disclaimer: The following content is provided for informative purposes only. We do not take any responsibility for attempting to follow it.

2 Disassembly

Attention

Be careful while cutting! Cut only the outside plastic and avoid sticking metal tools inside.

Wear gloves and eyewear to protect yourself.

If possible isolate the tip of the screwdriver with electrical tape.

Warning

If you see sparks, smoke, or even fire, toss the pack into a sand filled container and cover it with additional sand until the pack is safe to touch again.

Do not attempt to reuse the damaged pack. Dispose it correctly according the the local regulations.

Wise advice

Be careful while handling the pack. Avoid the use of brute force. Be patient and make multiple small cuts to the enclosure.

2.1. OPENING BATTERY PACKS

Use pliers or wire cutters to cut around the plastic casing. If there are openings in the plastic shell use a screwdriver to pry them open.

Click to enlarge image

Attention

In some cases, silicone based adheasives could have been used to seat the cells inside of the shell. Detach the batteries from the glue with the help of a non conductive tool.

2.2. REMOVE THE CELLS FROM THE SHELL

Once the pack has been cut and pried opened, carefully remove the cells and electronics from the shell. 

 

Click to enlarge image

Warning

Cut one cable at a time to avoid short circuits!

Wise advice

You can keep the nickel tabs attached to make the future connections much easier and safer.

2.3. STRIP THE BATTERIES OF THE CABLES

With the help of a wire cutter or a pair of scissors, gently disconnect the cells from the electronics and from one and the other.

Inspect each battery. Make sure there are no dents, corrosion or scratches. If one cell presents any of these signs, do not use it and dispose of it following your local regulations.

3 Preparation

Wise advice

Some manufacturers like Molicel, Samsung and sometimes LG list the capacity of the cell directly in its code. For example:

 

INR18650-M35A - Molicel 18650 High Capacity 3500mAh Cell

INR18650-30Q - Samsung High Capacity, High Discharge, 3000mAh Cell

3.1. SORTING THE CELLS

Locate the manufacturer's cell code printed on the side and find their original capacity in mA; this will be useful to compare to values the battery currently has vs. the original capacity.

Search for the cell datasheet on google, or check the values directly on reputable websites like Nkon and Second Life Storage's Cell Database. 

 

Here are a few examples of cell codes and the relative breakdown.

 

NCR18650GA - Sanyo 18650 High Capacity 3350mAh Cell

US18650VTC5C - Sony 18650 High Current 2500mAh Cell

Attention

If a cell measures between 1.9 and 2.9 there is a chance it might be still salvable. Proceed to step 3.1 to check how.

Warning

If a cell measures 0V do not attempt to recharge it. There is a high chance the cell is faulty or damaged and might overheat or vent if charged.

3.2. MEASURING THE CELL'S VOLTAGE

Using a multimeter, measure the voltage of each battery you have sourced.

 

A LiIon battery has a discharge curve that ranges between 2.9v and 4.2v.

A fully charged LiIon cell measures 4.2v with a tolerance of 0.05v.

An half charged LiIon cell measures from 3.5-3.7v.

A fully discharged LiIon cell tipically measures 2.9v.

 

These values will differ for other cell chemistries like LifePo4 and certain types of LiPo batteries.

Download the manufacturer's cell's datasheet to check its chemestry and discharge curve.

 

Separate the batteries in two groups: above 2.9V and under 2.9V.

4 Testing

Wise advice

This will take a while depending on how much power was left in the battery.

Warning

Charging should be done in a safe environment, away from potential fire hazards. Ideally inside of a fire resistant LiPo bag.

 

Do not leave the batteries unattended, frequently check if the cell's temperature and if it gets uncomfortably hot, disconnect the battery immediately, put it in a sand filled container and cover it with additional sand until the pack is safe to touch again. 

Dispose it correctly according the the local regulations.

4.1. Reviving batteries (under 2.9v)

You can try to revive batteries that are under 2.9V by connecting them to a bench power supply set to constant current constant voltage mode.

Set a maximum current threshold of 0.5A and a maximum charge voltage of 3.0V.

Wait till the power supply shows no current being delivered to the cell.

 

At this point you can proceed with the steps described for the batteries above 2.9V.

Wise advice

By following the cell datasheet you can also approximately calculate how many cycles your cell has done.

Warning

Charging should be done in a safe environment, away from potential fire hazards. Ideally inside of a fire resistant LiPo bag.

 

Do not leave the batteries unattended, frequently check if the cell's temperature and if it gets uncomfortably hot, disconnect the battery immediately, put it in a sand filled container and cover it with additional sand until the pack is safe to touch again. 

Dispose it correctly according the the local regulations.

4.2. Testing battery capacity (above 2.9V)

Connect the batteries to the charger and start charging at about 500mA.

Once the batteries are fully charged, drain each one using discharge function of your charger.

At the end of the procedure your charger should show you the capacity of each battery.

Write on the cell its capacity and compare the original capacity listed in the datasheet.

Wise advice

If you are working with a large number of cells, an Excel sheet would be useful to keep track of all the values.

4.3. MARKING THE CELLS

Use a marker and number each battery (#1, #2, …). note as well on each cell the voltage (V) and capacity (mAh)  you just measured.

 

Click to enlarge image

Wise advice

Ideally, batteries in good condition that are stored properly should loose 3% of their capacity in the first 24h and after that 1-2% per month.

4.4. STORAGE TEST

Charge the cells and store them safely for at least 1 weeks. After one week, using a multimeter check the voltage of each battery.

If the cells measures below 4.1V, it indicates that the battery cannot hold its charge.

5 Usage

Wise advice

Keep in mind that batteries that have a small capacity (under 1500 mAh) can still be used to power some devices that don’t require a lot of power.

Warning

If you are planning to put your recycled cells in parallel, rember that cells with different capacities will charge each other with potentially dangerous amounts of current.

When connecting them together be sure they have the same approximate capacity and voltage!

 

If you plan to connect them in series, be sure to use a bms with balancing function and try to match the cell's capacities as much as possible.

Attention

If you don’t plan to immediately put the batteries to use store them at around 50/60% of their charge in a dry place.

5.1. FINAL STEP

At this point you can select batteries for the various uses you may have for them.

 

You can easily do so by dividing the battery’s capacity (mAh) by the estimated consumption of the circuit (mA), this will give you an estimate of how many hours a cell might last in that specific application.

 

Here is a list of how much power different LEDs in our products use:

Nichia NS2L757AT SMD-LED with PCB (Star): 180mA

Cree XP-G2 R2 SMD-LED with PCB: 700mA

7x Samsung LM281B Square 15 High-Performance: 1500mA Max

 

Following the given list, a cell with a capacity of 2200mAh will last:

12hrs with the Nichia LED

3hrs with the Cree LED

1hr 20m with the Samsung LED
